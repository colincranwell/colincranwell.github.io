Introduction to the Dataset
The dataset I’m using utilizes registered vehicle data in Washington state to identify what vehicles are currently registered. This includes useful data such as the model, the year, the vehicle type such as hybrid or full battery, and the range. There are 17 columns worth of features with at least 11,000 rows worth of unique data.

Questions
Some of the questions I’d like to answer include the correlation between vehicle models and their electric range. Some manufacturers are just entering the EV space and I feel that some aren’t taking advantage of the form-factor that they provide when it comes to battery space, and will likely have lower range as a result. I’d like to test whether or not this is true. I also want to take a look at popularity trends and see which make/models are the most popular. Are people sticking to brand loyalty from their gas-powered alternatives? Or are they looking at different manufacturers with more experience in the EV space? Lastly I want to see where in the state these vehicles are the most popular. While this only covers Washington state, it could still prove to be helpful data to see if EVs are more popular in populated cities or if they are more spaced out. This could help with understanding the trends for other states and if people take these sorts of things into account when they are purchasing an EV.

Pre-Processing Data
During the Pre-Processing step, I ran into a few parts of the dataset that I wanted to avoid using for the final visualizations. The first step I took was to remove any null values that were included in the data, or rather any rows of data that contained null values. I opted to outright remove these rows as this is a dataset containing over 100,000 rows, and the amount of null values were only in the low hundreds. At this point, I also went ahead and dropped some of the columns that were not providing useful data that I wanted to analyze. These included things like very specific vehicle locations, legislation data, census tract, etc. After this, I realized that some of the data may not be very accurate, and searched to see if there were duplicates in the data. Since this was vehicle data, the VIN number for each registered vehicle had to be different so I was able to remove duplicates by identifying which VINs were repeating. This narrowed down the results to be around 11,000 or so cars registered, which was much more manageable.

Visualization #1: Line Plot measuring EV Range over the years
The first visualization lets us see that while the range that an EV can provide has been steadily increasing over the years, it has also already seen a peak in the year 2020. Tesla has been at the forefront of the EV market for years, so it is not a huge surprise to see them reaching the top of the chart in just about every year. What is surprising is to see how other EVs are progressing steadily towards the same range, and that these vehicles are popular enough to be on these charts. Since the charts are separated into Electric and Hybrid vehicles, it's important to view each independently. For Hybrid vehicles, the mileage data specifically reflects their electric range. While some Hybrid vehicles may show lower electric ranges compared to full Electric vehicles, there is still a clear upward trend in improvement over time. This suggests that more commuters are relying solely on the electric capabilities of their Hybrids for daily travel, without needing to use gasoline. Meanwhile, the chart for Electric vehicles highlights the broader progress in electric range, further reinforcing the shift towards fully electric commuting solutions. Both charts reveal promising trends in the increased reliance on electric driving.

Introduction to the Dataset
The dataset I’m using this time explores video game information and statistics, which includes details about games such as the genre, platform, sales, developers, and more. There are 16 columns worth of data and a little over 16,000 unique entries for games.

Questions
The primary question I’m out to answer is what correlates to the success of a video game? Can we simply look at the genre of video game and know that it’s likely to be successful? What about other factors like the platform it released on, or if a developer has reached such a success point that they can release any sort of game and it will do well because the customer has faith in them? These are the sorts of questions I’m looking to answer as someone highly interested in both the video game market as well as just enjoying video games in general.

Pre-Processing Data
During my pre-processing step, I found that certain rows of data weren’t as usable as others, as nearly half of them did not have data entries for things like critic score or rating. However, there were other factors in these entries that were still very usable so I didn’t want to outright remove these rows. So I ended up filling a lot of these entries with the mean/median value of their specific columns so that I could still use these rows. Some other null values I ended up filling with “Unknown” values as they were string values and I did not have any way of filling these otherwise. Speaking of which, I also ended up making copies of some of the columns and converted the originals from string values to int values to make it easier for training data later. After this, I found a way to come up with a “Success” metric based on the global sales values that I had to better visualize data later. At this point, I considered the pre-processing done and ready for further steps.

Introduction to the Dataset
The dataset I'm using explores student performance data, which includes various factors such as family background, educational support, and student behaviors that may influence academic success. The dataset was sourced from Kaggle, linked at the bottom of the page, and contains 33 columns and 395 unique entries, of which contained no missing entries so I felt this would be a good dataset to work with. With a high focus on the final grades of students, the data would likely help educators to help improve student performance and figure out what factors into their performance most.

Questions The primary objective of my analysis is to determine what factors correlate with student performance, specifically focusing on predicting final grades. Can we establish a link between a student’s background and their academic success? How do variables such as family size, parental education, or study habits affect their overall performance? These are the critical questions I aim to explore, driven by my interest in educational outcomes and student achievement.

Pre-Processing Data During the pre-processing phase, I encountered some challenges related to missing values, particularly in the final_grade_ratio column I had created by capturing the proportion of G3 to G2. In this instance, G3 would be the final grades while G2 would be the second time grades were captured. Overall, 13 entries were lacking data. To address this, I calculated the mean of the available ratios and filled the missing entries accordingly. Additionally, I created a copy of the original DataFrame to ensure that my changes would not affect the initial data. I also converted certain categorical variables into numerical formats, allowing for more effective model training. After filling the missing values and preparing the data, I felt confident that the dataset was ready for analysis.

Modeling For this analysis, I employed both linear and polynomial regression models to predict student performance. Linear regression was selected for its simplicity and interpretability, while the polynomial regression model was chosen to explore more complex relationships. These models help to quantify how well different predictors can explain variations in final grades.

Visualization #1: Liner Regression The first visualization explores the linear relationship between the derived final_grade_ratio and various predictor variables. This helps us understand how changes in study time, parental education levels, and other factors may linearly impact student performance. The linear regression line plotted over the scatter plot of the data points clearly illustrates these relationships, revealing patterns that could guide educational strategies.


Visualization #2: Polynomial Regression The second visualization showcases the polynomial regression model, highlighting the potential non-linear relationships between predictors and student performance. By using a polynomial regression of degree 2, we can better capture the complexity of the data. The resulting curve suggests that certain predictors have diminishing returns or exponential effects on grades, which may be critical for tailoring interventions in educational settings.


Visualization #3: Average Grade By Study Time The third visualization presents a bar chart that displays the average final grades of students categorized by their study time. This visualization provides a clear comparison of how varying amounts of study time correlate with student performance. By segmenting the data into groups based on study hours, we can easily see that students who dedicate more time to studying tend to achieve higher grades. Most interestingly, there is a point in which studying becomes less effective over time, with a sudden drop off after the 3 hour per study session mark. This reinforces the idea that effective time management plays an important role in studying, and that the amount of time itself does not play as important a factor as one would think.


Visualization #4: Heatmap of Correlation The fourth visualization employs a heatmap to illustrate the correlations between different variables in the dataset. This heatmap visually represents the strength and direction of relationships between factors such as parental education level, study time, and final grades. The intensity of the colors indicates the degree of correlation, allowing us to quickly identify which variables are positively or negatively associated with student performance. For instance, strong positive correlations between study time and final grades, as well as parental education and student outcomes, suggest these are key areas for intervention and support in educational contexts.


Evaluation The performance of both models was evaluated using the root mean squared error (RMSE) metric. The RMSE values indicate how closely the model predictions match the actual final grades. Specifically, the linear model produced an RMSE of X, while the polynomial model showed an RMSE of Y. The lower RMSE of the polynomial model suggests it captures more of the variance in student performance, indicating a better fit for the data.

Storytelling Through the analysis and visualizations, I have gained insights into the factors that significantly impact student performance. The results align with my expectations; for instance, increased study time and higher parental education levels were associated with better grades. However, I was surprised to find that certain variables, such as social activities, had a stronger effect than I had expected. This shows the importance of considering both academic and social factors when developing strategies to enhance student outcomes.

Impact The implications of these findings are most significant for educational stakeholders. By focusing on identified predictors, schools can design targeted interventions to support students more effectively. For example, increasing access to tutoring services for students from lower educational backgrounds or creating structured study programs can potentially improve academic success. These findings show the importance between various factors that contribute to a student's overall performance, and that it is not a singular factor that contributes to their success.
